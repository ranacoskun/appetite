Introduction

As three individuals with diverse tastes in food, we've established a magazine named "APPETITE" for fellow food enthusiasts who share our passion for exploring various cuisines. The name, which is quite self explanatory, is to underline the hunger for both the food and the knowledge about it.
Users can easily switch between various themes by utilizing the theme-switching button located at the bottom right of the screen. Our intention was to provide a comprehensive experience, capturing the essence of different periods through the chosen themes, while also infusing a touch of contemporary style. Throughout this design process, we carefully considered the user experience (UX) to ensure a seamless and engaging exploration for our readers.
Additionally, we've incorporated a "Meta Data Viewer" tool that lets the users explore the metadata embedded in the texts, revealing details about people, places, dishes, and more. It adds an extra layer of depth to the reading experience, allowing enthusiasts to delve into the intricacies of the content.

Organization of the Website

We have chosen 6 styles in total, from different periods of time. Here is the complete list of themes we have selected for each period:

1500 - 1800 Baroque
XIX Century Russian Constructivism
First half of XX Century Art Deco
Second half of XX Century Pop-art
Late XX Century – start of XXI Century Banksy
2030 Minimalism

The selection of switchable themes for our magazine is rooted in a deliberate effort to provide readers with a rich and immersive experience that spans various historical and artistic periods. Each theme has been chosen for its unique aesthetic and cultural significance, aligning with the respective time frame:

Baroque (1500 - 1800): Chosen for its opulence and grandeur, Baroque adds a touch of historical richness, allowing readers to explore a period marked by elaborate details and dramatic expression.

Constructivism (XIX Century): Reflecting the industrial and geometric trends of the 19th century, Constructivism adds a sense of innovation and forward-thinking design to the reading experience.

Art Deco (First half of XX Century): Art Deco, with its luxurious and glamorous style, brings a sense of elegance from the early 20th century, offering readers a glimpse into a period marked by geometric shapes and sleek design.

Pop-art (Second half of XX Century): The vibrant and lively aesthetic of Pop-art captures the energy and dynamism of the mid-20th century, celebrating popular culture and modernity.

Minimalism (2030): Chosen for its simplicity and focus on essential forms, Minimalism represents a forward-looking theme, embracing modern design principles and offering readers a glimpse into a potential future aesthetic.

By providing a range of switchable themes, we aim to cater to diverse tastes and preferences, offering readers the opportunity to explore and appreciate the evolution of art and design across different time periods. Each theme adds a unique dimension to the magazine, enhancing the overall reading experience.

The MMMM Framework

M-Metadata:Our Metadata Viewer is a tool designed to offer readers a deeper understanding of the content within our magazine. As readers explore articles, they can use the Metadata Viewer to unveil additional layers of information. This tool provides a list of entities, revealing details about places, people, concepts, and dishes mentioned in the articles.

M-Map:We are using maps for geo-referencing. In our magazine the articles are linked to particular locations like a city or a country. Users are able to explore and move through articles using an interactive map feature.

M- Magazine:We're not just focusing on one magazine; we've set up a system to regularly and reliably cook up magazine issues, especially for food enthusiasts, ensuring each one serves a delicious blend of quality and variety.
We have added 3 issues for 3 countries that are dear to our hearts; Italy, Turkey and Kazakhstan. Every issue deals with specific cousines of the countries mentioned.

M-Mixing:We Mix the elements of maps, our Metadata Viewer, and the core essence of our Magazine. The fusion of these components creates a dynamic and interactive reading experience for our audience.

This project took shape as part of the Information Modelling And Web Technologies course, and according to our professor’s requirements of the project, we made sure that our website had the following feature:
1. Automatic:
- Streamlining the process, theme selection automatically applies typographical and layout choices, ensuring quick and consistent design implementation.
2. Believable:
- Designs are not just visually impactful but grounded in historical accuracy, with invented styles aligning plausibly with aesthetic principles.
3. Complete:
- The project manages and displays all aspects of the HTML document, providing a comprehensive and immersive user experience.
4. Deliberate:
- Every design choice is intentional, planned, and rooted in a thoughtful understanding of the historical theme, contributing to a coherent and engaging narrative.

Covers

Despite being an online magazine, we've retained the cherished tradition of magazine covers to pay homage to the tangible and visually captivating nature of traditional print publications. For each issue and theme, we've meticulously crafted unique covers that serve as visual gateways to the content within.

These covers not only capture the essence of the specific theme but also act as inviting glimpses into the diverse narratives presented in each issue. Whether it's the opulence of the Baroque era, the geometric precision of Constructivism, or the vibrant energy of Pop-art, our covers serve as visual ambassadors, setting the tone for the immersive experience that awaits readers.

By incorporating covers into our online magazine, we aim to blend the familiarity of traditional print aesthetics with the dynamic possibilities of the digital realm, providing readers with a visually compelling and immersive entry point to the world of each thematic issue.

Styles in detail

Baroque

The Baroque period, which spanned the 17th to 18th centuries, originated in Italy as a reaction to the Mannerism that dominated the late Italian Renaissance. The term "baroque" is derived from the Portuguese word for "oddly shaped pearl" and was initially used derogatorily. It has since been widely used to describe the period, known for its exuberance, theatricality, and emotional expression.

The Baroque era encompassed various art forms, including painting, sculpture, architecture, decorative arts, and music. It was characterized by dramatic and emotional artistic expressions, and it was embraced by countries absorbed in the Counter-Reformation, particularly the Roman Catholic Church.

The period's most notable practitioners were artists such as Annibale Carracci, Caravaggio, and Gian Lorenzo Bernini, as well as composers like Claudio Monteverdi, Johann Sebastian Bach, and George Frideric Handel. The Baroque period was a response to the idealism of the Renaissance and the forced nature of Mannerism, and it was encouraged by the Catholic Church and absolute monarchies in Europe to communicate religious themes and glorify their own divine greatness.

Some of the notable typographical features of the Baroque period included:
- Little or no bracketing on serifs, which are the curved connections between a stem and a serif.
- Ornate borders and typographical elements were common, in line with the prevailing artistic trends of the Baroque period.

These changes in typography were influenced by the broader artistic developments of the Baroque era, which was known for its abundant detail, strong contrast between light and dark, bright colors, and emotional drama.

MARGINS

During the Baroque period, certain books were printed with larger margins as a cost-saving measure in response to the high expenses associated with printing. However, it's important to note that this was not a standardized practice for all prints. There was notable variation, and our observations reveal that some books had larger margins than others, reflecting the diverse approaches taken by publishers during that era.

ORNAMENT

“Representations of the natural world, as well as motifs derived from human and animal forms, were popular decorative features. The most widespread form of Baroque floral decoration was a running scroll, often combined with acanthus – a stylised version of a real plant of the same name. A late 17th-century tankard in our collection features lavish floral decoration. The leaves of the flowers have been turned into scrolling foliage, while the flowers themselves have striped petals, likely to represent a tulip, another key motif of Baroque art.”

OUR APPROACH

In integrating the Baroque style into our website, we've drawn inspiration from the ornamental elements widely utilized in art during the Baroque period. For the text we have chosen to use a commonly used typeface called Garamond which is very similar to Jason, and we tried to mimic the layout of our references. For the general theme and visuals, we've looked to contemporary fashion, particularly Versace's (I ♡ BAROQUE Collection) distinctive prints, and modern art (Jacques-Louis David Meets Kehinde Wiley) that employs Baroque style, for a more modern take. With a primary focus on user experience (UX), we've made concerted efforts to enhance legibility, ensuring a seamless and visually engaging browsing experience for our users.

Constructivism

Constructivism was the most influential modern art movement in twentieth century Russia. With its aesthetic roots fixed firmly in the Suprematism movement, Constructivism came fully to the fore as the art of a young Soviet Union after the revolution of 1917. The movement was conceived of out of a need for a new aesthetic language; one benefitting of a progressive new era in Soviet socialist history. Constructivism also borrowed elements of other European avant-gardes, notably Cubism and Futurism, and at its heart was the idea that artmaking should be approached as a process of cerebral “construction”.

IDEOLOGY

Constructivism became closely aligned with the idea of agitprop (agitational propaganda) which applied to an artwork that aimed to educate and indoctrinate its audience to the prevailing Bolshevik cause. By employing arrangements of basic industrial geometric forms and colors the new Soviet Union would be represented by the new languages of industry and modernity. However, most of the country were semi/illiterate land workers, and these remote communities were reached through Agit Trains. These featured (amongst other things) purpose-built cinemas which delivered the Bolshevik message through unambiguous propaganda films, known as agitki.

Colors and layout

Pure geometric forms, linearity, symmetry, repetition, simple, sans-serif fonts, the dominance of red and black, photomontage. Using these elements, Constructivists would create a graphic design style that rejected all artifice and that we still associate with post-revolution Russia today. Its purposes included informing a population of mostly illiterate people about the politics of the new government. That was the reason for one of the its main characteristics: font manipulation in order to give each part of the text the characteristics (e.g. a shape or colour) that reflected its importance within the message.

First half of XX Century - Art Deco

Art Deco is an era in art that took place in the years 1900 - 1945 and it is easily recognizable by its sense of glamour, elegance and optimism for the future (Blackman, 2020). This period was an important time for visual art, architecture, interior design and theatre, but the real defining element of this era was in graphic design and typography. The 1920s hold the year when the American type designer W.A. Dwiggins originally used the term "Graphic Design" in 1922, which falls within the 1920s.

The name inspiration for Art Deco came from where the style was exhibited first; at the Exposition Internationale des Arts Décoratifs et Industriels Modernes, held in Paris in 1925. The Art Deco style was a direct opposition response to the style of Art Nouveau which came before it. Art Deco is primarily geometric, sleek, and features hard edges and straight, smooth lines, whereas Art Nouveau was highly ornate, with many expressive lines and organic shapes. Both of these styles aimed for elegance, but they did so in quite different ways.

The style characteristics of Art Deco include: (Schenker, 2018) (Johnson, 2018)

  • Zigzags, chevrons, zigurat patterns
  • Sunrise or sunburst motifs
  • Geometric, symmetrical patterns
  • Straight lines with hard edges
  • Intricate line art
  • Symbolizing wealth and sophistication

    • PHILOSOPHY

    The period was one of dramatic technological change, social upheaval and political and economic crises, of bewildering contrasts and apocalyptic visions. From the Roaring Twenties to the Depression, the inexorable spread of capitalism was mirrored by that of Fascist and Communist totalitarian regimes, while remorseless globalization was accompanied by isolationist nationalism, At the same time, the spread of mass-produced consumer goods, accompanied by the perfection of promotional methods to generate demand, prioritized visual appeal in the seduction of the would-be consumer. Never was fantasy so functionally necessary for survival, whether to industry or the individual.

    “Art Deco was not a movement. It had no founder, no manifesto, and no philosophy. It simply happened because designers and decorators in Paris during the period after the First World War were stimulated by the demands of a restructured society. Unlike the idealists of the Bauhaus or De Stijl, they had no intention of imposing rigorous new lifestyles, but in the process of their work, these French designers created a broad new style, which rapidly became popular elsewhere. This style is instantly recognizable because everything was designed to go together and make a complete ensemble; also it is encapsulated so neatly in the period between the wars and the Second World War forced a complete stylistic break in Europe, causing a sharp separation between Art Deco and its successors.

    The clearest hallmark of Art Deco is its geometry, which was largely derived from Cubism. Everything from flowers to the human form became angular. Shapes became bolder and simpler as geometry took over. Subjects that were particularly well suited to this treatment featured frequently and have now come to be called the symbols of Art Deco. The sunburst with its clearly defined circle surrounded by radiating lines is one of them; the ziggurat is another, as is the formalized fountain motif with its arc-shapes.

    Other important aspects of Art Deco were streamlining and jazziness. Speed was considered to be one of the great marvels of the twentieth century- ‘speed is our God’, wrote the Futurist poet Marinetti-and the sleek lines - imposed by the laws of aerodynamics became more and more a feature of the design.”

    TYPEFACE

    The design direction of Art Deco was geometric and simplified. Compared to previous ornate styles, the typefaces felt less feminine due to the addition of thick strokes in place of elaborate flourishes. To keep the lettering intriguing and competitive with the intricate Art Nouveau typefaces that people were accustomed to seeing, type designers of the era employed a great deal of variation in line weights and diagonal lines in their designs.

    Style characteristics of Art Deco typefaces are:

  • Using a sans serif base, thick strokes and contrasting thin strokes
  • Alternating lines or fills
  • Exaggerated x-height, generally tall lettering
  • Characters are very straight-up and down
  • High crossbars evoke the thought of skyscrapers and futuristic architecture
  • All capital letters, some popular typefaces did not even include lowercase letters

    • The overall style of Art Deco typography was largely set by Adolphe Jean-Marie Mouron, a Ukrainian-French artist known under the pseudonym Cassandre. He was a poster designer, costume maker, painter, typographer, and lecturer. Inspired by cubism and surrealism, his works stood out and garnered a lot of attention during the early 1920s in Paris.

    In the context of the "APPETITE" magazine's Art Deco theme, the Peignot font is employed. Peignot is a sans-serif typeface designed by Cassandre in 1937 for the French foundry Deberny & Peignot. Its geometric and stylized characters align seamlessly with the Art Deco aesthetic, reminiscent of Cassandre's innovative approach. The design is unique in that the lowercase contains a mix of uppercase and lowercase letterforms. Its geometric and stylized characters add a touch of sophistication. The font size is carefully adjusted for readability while maintaining the iconic Art Deco aesthetic.

    We chose X font for our article page. Although there are mostly typefaces contain only capital letters in the Art Deco style, we used the X font, which is the most reasonable in terms of readability and user experience.

    MARGINS

    During the Art Deco period, the margins in printed magazines' texts were often well-defined but not strictly standardized. The design ethos of Art Deco favored a sense of luxury and sophistication, reflected in the careful consideration of space and layout. Margins were typically moderate, allowing for a balance between text and empty space on the page.

    Art Deco magazines embraced a departure from the elaborate and often asymmetrical layouts of earlier periods. Instead, a more streamlined approach was adopted, with clean lines and geometric shapes influencing the design. While the margins were not excessively wide, they provided a sufficient buffer around the text, contributing to a visually pleasing and organized composition.

    This design trend aimed to convey a sense of modernity and elegance. Although specific measurements varied, the overall emphasis was on achieving a harmonious balance between content and white space, aligning with the principles of Art Deco aesthetics.

    Embracing the symmetry of Art Deco, we use wide/broad margins to create a sleek and streamlined look. The aspect ratio is balanced, and page sizes are optimized for digital platforms, ensuring a visually pleasing and immersive reading experience.

    GRAPHIC DESIGN

    Another medium where Art Deco found its expression is graphic design. Elegant posters, illustrations, photographs, and magazine covers created in this fashion often adorned pages of famous magazines such as Vogue, Vanity Fair, Harper’s Bazaar, and La Gazette du Bon Ton, which helped popularize the style even more.

    CASSANDRE, an artist who, during the 30's, will occupy the first place in the world of advertising posters. Through his famous works, posters for the Nord Express trains, Étoile du Nord, the Normandie and L'atlantique liners, the well known Dubo, Dubon, Dubonnet triptych, his typographic creations, or even the Yves Saint-Laurent logo, CASSANDRE was not only, according to the words of his friend Blaise Cendrars, one of the most fervent animators of modern life, but above all, one of the most outstanding founders of graphic design.

    COLOR

    The colors used in Art Deco designs were also a defining characteristic of the era. Metallic colors such as bronze and gold were common in design, especially in graphic design and typography. In general, rich, vibrant, kitschy colors were used quite a bit for fashion and decoration.

    Red, black and silver is a typical color combinations, the red and black providing a background of geometric shapes and the silver acting as a separating highlight or defining ziggurats and other geometric motifs. Sharp colors like acid green or orange were also popular as they could help to make a shape stand out better. On the whole, the color spectrum of Art Deco is a bright one, saved from garishness by supreme elegance. Vertical planes, horizontal planes and clearly defined curves became structure and decoration in one, and this basic geometry got stricter as the years went by.

    We used … The color palette, including gold, dark, and deep hues, echoes the glamour associated with the movement, while narrow margins and balanced aspect ratios create a sleek and streamlined look.

    CONTEMPORARY WEBSITE EXAMPLES

    Pop Art

    Pop art emerged as an art movement during the 1950s in America and Britain, reaching its peak in the 1960s. It began as a revolt against the dominant approaches to art and culture, with young artists feeling that the art taught in schools and exhibited in museums did not represent the reality of the world around them. As a result, they turned to sources such as Hollywood movies, advertising, product packaging, pop music, and comic books for their imagery. The movement presented a challenge to traditions of fine art by including imagery from popular and mass culture, such as advertising, comic books, and mundane mass-produced objects. Pop art aimed to use images of popular culture in art, emphasizing the banal or kitschy elements of any culture, most often through the use of irony.

    Roy Lichtenstein was a key figure in the Pop art movement, known for his comic book-inspired style and innovative use of Ben-Day dots. Although there are many influential artists in utilizing pop art style, we have chosen Lichtenstein as our biggest reference.

    TYPEFACE
    Typography featured prominently in Lichtenstein's early paintings, as he would appropriate written texts from comic book speech bubbles, sometimes altering or omitting them to create a new context for the words within his paintings.
    The typeface he uses is a type of sans serif and all capital. In some of his work we see the use of italic as well.

    COLOR
    Roy Lichtenstein's use of color in his paintings was indeed characterized by the prominent use of primary colors. He often utilized a limited color palette, typically consisting of primary colors such as red, yellow, and blue, with black outlines. His sophisticated compositions of surfaces were enriched by bold colors, letters and other symbols.

    “Roy Lichtenstein’s drowning, crying and anxious ‘girls’ were depicted as idealised blonde bombshells that spoke to contemporary beauty standards and parodied her social expectations. By singling out the troubled woman in a comic strip, Lichtenstein immortalised her pain and poked fun at the modern image of the ‘perfect’ woman who was naïve and completely at the mercy of her emotions.”

    OUR APPROACH

    While Lichtenstein successfully employed capital letters for every word in his paintings where small texts were used, we have chosen not to continue this practice. This decision is driven by the fact that our texts are significantly longer, and we want to optimize the user experience (UX).

    Inspired by Lichtenstein's frequent depiction of women in his paintings, we incorporated visual elements showcasing women on our website.

    As for the color palette, again taking into account user experience (UX), we expanded it beyond primary colors, drawing inspiration from influential artists who share a similar style, like Tadanori Yokoo.

    Banksy

    This style is inspired by stencil street art that is following Banksy artist. Banksy is one of the brightest figures in modern art since 1980s. His art is famous for reflecting problems in the world and society.

    Most of his works are quite dark so we tried to recreate his vibe by using the fonts that resemble stencil art style and use main colours of his works: black, red and grey. Also, we trying to mimic one of his murals by placing concrete textures on the background.

    Hence Banksy is a modern artist we decided to implement best practices of web design for our layout that are widely used nowadays.

    Stencil art is one of humanity’s oldest creative forms. Some of our species’ first artists used stencil art techniques when they placed their hands on cave walls and blew ground minerals over them, coating the rock in blooms of red or black pigment and leaving behindtheir ghostly palmprints.

    Fast-forward some 30,000 years and stencil art techniques remain essentially unchanged. Using a sheet of cardboard, plastic, or metal with a pattern or letters cut out is surprisingly versatile, allowing craftspeople to color cloth, illustrate manuscripts, print t-shirts, and create some fantastic street art.
